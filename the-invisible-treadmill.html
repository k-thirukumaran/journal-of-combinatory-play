The Invisible Treadmill

Why your brain cannot detect the overload it is in, why it rewards you for staying there, and why every new tool makes it worse.

This is not a moral failing. It is the predictable output of biological systems operating exactly as designed — in an environment they were never designed for.

CHAPTER I

You are not weak.
Your hardware is running perfectly.

Something is wrong. You can feel it vaguely — a background hum of too much, a sense that work never really ends, that thinking never quite reaches the depth it used to. And yet you function. You process. You deliver. So the feeling gets filed away.

This document is about why that filing happens, why it is biologically inevitable, and why the tools you are now using to cope are making the underlying problem structurally worse — while generating the neurological sensation of progress.

Start with three observations you have almost certainly made and then forgotten.

You are at a loud party. The noise is overwhelming — a wall of sound. Twenty minutes in, you have a conversation you can hear clearly. What changed? The room is equally loud. Your auditory baseline recalibrated. Your brain stopped treating the ambient noise as information and filed it as background.

Now here is the part people miss: if someone nearby quietly says your name — something genuinely important — it is now harder to hear them than it would have been in silence. You have not only filtered the noise. You have filtered the signals that live at the same volume as the noise.

This is not selective attention. This is perceptual baseline recalibration — a hard physical property of how sensation works. And it happens to your cognitive load in exactly the same way.

You hurt your lower back three years ago. For weeks it was all you could think about. Then it became a background presence. Then it became how your back just feels. You stopped describing yourself as someone in pain. You started describing yourself as someone who just has a bit of a bad back.

Nothing healed. The signal did not go away. Your perceptual system recalibrated its baseline upward and the pain became the new normal. On the day your back is worse than usual, you notice. On the day it is exactly as bad as it has been for three years, you do not.

Now substitute: cognitive demands, meeting load, interruption frequency, unfinished work. The mechanism is identical. The environment does not improve. The pain just stops being detectable as pain.

You joined a team two years ago. In month one you noticed things: the meetings ran over, priorities shifted unpredictably, no one seemed to own outcomes clearly. You had observations. You asked questions. Then you stopped.

Not because the problems were fixed. Because they became the texture of the environment rather than deviations from it. Your perceptual contrast — the gap between what you expected and what you saw — eroded. New joiners today still notice these things. You tell them "that's just how it works here."

This is the most consequential version of the phenomenon: the signal you stop detecting is not just your discomfort. It is the organisational dysfunction itself. It compounds invisibly while everyone in the system has recalibrated around it.

These three observations describe the same mechanism operating at different scales. The next two chapters explain the biology behind it — with precision, with the caveats the research actually demands, and with the implications for how you work and lead.

Organisations have become so acclimated to endless work in progress and fragmented attention that these conditions have become the operational norm. People adapt. Over time the overloaded state simply becomes what work feels like — not a problem to be solved, but the texture of work itself.

CHAPTER II

Mechanism One:
The Fading Lens

The first hardwired mechanism is not psychological or cultural. It is a physical law of how sensation works, formulated in the 19th century and robustly confirmed across every sensory modality studied since.

The Weber-Fechner Law

Ernst Heinrich Weber (1834) and Gustav Theodor Fechner (1860) established what is now called the Weber-Fechner Law: the just noticeable difference (JND) between two stimuli is not a fixed amount — it is a constant proportion of the starting intensity.

Fechner's extension formalised this as a logarithmic relationship. If a physical stimulus doubles, quadruples, or multiplies eightfold, your subjective perception of the change increases only by the same small fixed amount each time. Physical intensity climbs geometrically. Perceived sensation climbs arithmetic — slowly, by the same small step regardless of how large the underlying change becomes.

Interactive Visualisation — The Logarithmic Perception Gap
10

At a baseline load of 10, you need an additional 2 units of demand to notice a difference. As your baseline rises, the increment required for detection grows proportionally — while your absolute headroom shrinks.

Weber, E.H. (1834). De Pulsu, Resorptione, Auditu et Tactu. Leipzig. · Fechner, G.T. (1860). Elemente der Psychophysik. Leipzig: Breitkopf und Härtel. · Algom, D. (2021). The Weber-Fechner law: A misnomer that persists but should go away. Psychological Review, 128(4), 757–765.
⚠ SCOPE NOTE: The law was established for physical sensory stimuli (weight, sound, light). Its extension to cognitive and emotional load is an evidence-informed analogy, not a direct mechanistic application. Recent work (Berkovich & Meiran, 2023, PLOS ONE) shows emotional stimuli also conform to Weber's Law in JND tasks — giving the analogy empirical grounding — but the cognitive domain is more complex than pure sensory discrimination.

The practical applications are everywhere once you see them. The decibel scale is built on it — sound engineers use logarithmic scales because that is how hearing works. Pharmacology dose-response curves approximate it. The f-stop system in photography is logarithmic for the same reason.

Weber's fraction — the ratio required for a just noticeable difference — is approximately 0.02 for lifted weights and 0.05 for auditory tones. It is not perfectly constant and shows deviations at extremes. But the principle is robust: you detect relative change, not absolute change.

A single candle transforms a completely dark room. Its effect is enormous — the room goes from total darkness to navigable. A second candle adds noticeably to the brightness. By the time you have ten candles in a brightly lit room, adding an eleventh is barely perceptible.

The eleventh candle produces exactly as many photons as the first. The change is identical in physical terms. The perceived change is nearly nothing.

Now replace candles with demands, meetings, interruptions, unread messages. The tenth demand added to an already full plate is physically the same as the first demand added to an empty one. Its perceptual impact is a fraction of the first — because your baseline has risen to meet it. You do not feel ten times as loaded. You feel roughly as loaded as you always do — because that is how the perceptual system works.

The Signal-Noise Asymmetry

The most consequential implication — and one that is almost entirely absent from management literature — is that perceptual recalibration cannot selectively filter only irrelevant noise.

When your baseline rises, everything rises with it. Genuinely important signals — a team member quietly deteriorating, a strategic risk compounding slowly, a subtle but persistent degradation in output quality — become indistinguishable from the background noise they live alongside.

The person is not choosing to ignore them. The perceptual contrast required to register them as distinct is simply no longer available. The antenna has been recalibrated to a noisier frequency band, and the faint but important signals are now below the detection threshold.

Reflection Prompt

Think of the last time someone new joined your team and noticed something about how you work that you no longer see. What was it? When did you stop seeing it? What else might you have stopped seeing at the same time?

Where the Model Breaks Down: The Burnout Cliff

Stevens' Power Law (1957) proposed that for extreme stimuli — particularly pain — the relationship is not logarithmic but exponential: small increases past a threshold feel dramatically more intense. This creates a non-linearity that has direct relevance to burnout and collapse.

Visualisation — The Adaptation Zone & The Cliff

For most of the range, logarithmic adaptation suppresses perception — the grey zone. Past a threshold, the response flips exponential — the cliff. Burnout appears sudden to observers because the sufferer has been in the logarithmic suppression zone, unaware of their own deterioration, until the threshold is crossed.

⚠ Stevens' law was formulated for sensory stimuli. Its direct application to psychological breakdown is inferential. The broader principle — that adaptation produces sudden discontinuities rather than gradual decline — is supported by allostatic load research and burnout literature, but claiming direct mechanistic equivalence overstates what the evidence shows.
Stevens, S.S. (1957). On the psychophysical law. Psychological Review, 64(3), 153–181. · Berkovich, R. & Meiran, N. (2023). Positive emotions conform to Weber's Law. PLOS ONE.
Stevens' Power Law: S = kIⁿ where n > 1 for pain (n ≈ 3.5) and n < 1 for most other modalities. The logarithmic law is a special case where n approaches 0 at the limit. The flip between regimes is relevant but the exact threshold for cognitive/emotional load cannot be specified from sensory research alone.

The mechanism in summary: Your perceptual system does not measure absolute levels — it measures proportional change from baseline. As baseline rises, your threshold for detecting further change rises with it. This is not resilience or adaptation in any useful sense. It is a fading lens: the more loaded the environment, the less able you are to detect that you are loaded. And crucially, the less able you are to detect the genuinely important signals that live in the same frequency band as the noise.

There is no internal alarm that fires when adaptation has gone too far. The mechanism that would detect the problem is the same mechanism that has been recalibrated by it.

CHAPTER III

Mechanism Two:
The Seeking Engine

The second mechanism operates at the neurochemical level. It explains why — even when people understand intellectually that they are overloaded — they keep processing, keep switching, keep checking. Understanding it requires correcting a widely repeated but imprecise claim.

Correcting the Dopamine Myth

ℹ A popular version of this idea states: "dopamine is an anticipation molecule, not a pleasure molecule — it is not released at the end of a reward." This is partially correct but meaningfully overstated, and the inaccuracy matters if the argument is to rest on solid ground.

Wolfram Schultz's foundational research (1997–1998) established what the system actually does. Dopamine neurons fire based on reward prediction errors — the gap between what was expected and what actually happened.

Visualisation — How the Dopamine Signal Shifts With Learning

Click each phase to explore how the signal evolves from naive reward to trained cue response. The key insight: once a reward is fully predictable, the dopamine signal migrates entirely to the cue. Certainty kills the signal. Uncertainty maintains it.

Schultz, W., Dayan, P., & Montague, P.R. (1997). A neural substrate of prediction and reward. Science, 275(5306), 1593–1599. · Schultz, W. (1998). Predictive reward signal of dopamine neurons. Journal of Neurophysiology, 80(1), 1–27.
This is among the most replicated findings in modern neuroscience. The prediction-error framework has been confirmed across species (rodents, primates, humans) using electrophysiology, fMRI, and pharmacological methods. The temporal difference learning model (Montague, Dayan, Sejnowski, 1996) provides the computational framework.

The precise and more defensible statement is this: the dopamine system is an uncertainty and prediction-error system. It responds most powerfully to unpredictable rewards and to cues that reliably predict uncertain outcomes. Fully certain, fully predicted outcomes generate the weakest dopamine response. This means the system is fundamentally oriented toward seeking — toward approaching uncertain rewards — rather than toward the hedonic experience of receiving them.

The Wanting-Liking Split

Jaak Panksepp's work on the SEEKING system provides a complementary framework. He proposed that dopaminergic circuitry running from the ventral tegmental area through the nucleus accumbens and medial prefrontal cortex constitutes a primary motivational system — mediating anticipatory eagerness, exploratory drive, and goal-directed approach behaviour.

The critical distinction is between wanting (the motivational drive to seek — substantially mediated by dopamine) and liking (the hedonic experience of pleasure — substantially mediated by opioid systems). These are neurologically separable. An organism can powerfully want something that produces little pleasure upon receipt. This dissociation explains compulsive seeking behaviour that persists despite producing no meaningful satisfaction.

Panksepp, J. (1998). Affective Neuroscience: The Foundations of Human and Animal Emotions. Oxford University Press. · Alcaro, A. & Panksepp, J. (2011). The SEEKING mind. Neuroscience & Biobehavioral Reviews, 35(9), 1805–1820.
⚠ CONTESTED: Lisa Feldman Barrett and others have argued that Panksepp's proposal that seven discrete hardwired emotional circuits are biologically basic goes beyond what the data currently supports. The SEEKING system concept is influential and broadly consistent with the Schultz prediction-error literature, but should be treated as a well-supported theoretical framework rather than settled neuroscience. Davis & Montag (2018, Frontiers in Neuroscience, 12, 1025) provides a balanced review of the evidence base.

The Cascading Dysfunctions

When the prediction-error system is placed inside a high-stimulation, high-interruption environment with multiple competing sources of unpredictable reward — every inbox, every notification, every AI output — several well-documented dysfunctions emerge predictably.

When a task feels large, vague, or temporally distant, the brain cannot form a clear reward prediction. Without that prediction signal, the dopamine motivational system stalls — a motivational flatline between intention and action.

The brain then migrates toward activities where the reward prediction is clearer and faster. Not because those activities are more valuable. Because the prediction signal is more available.

The brain is not wired for concurrent cognitive processing on multiple complex tasks. What we call multitasking is rapid task-switching — and each switch incurs a metabolic and attentional cost as the prefrontal cortex and striatum reorient.

Notifications and messages generate frequent small, unpredictable rewards — high-frequency dopamine activation. Meaningful tasks, with delayed and uncertain rewards, cannot compete neurologically without deliberate structural support.

Source: Earl Miller, MIT neuroscientist, cited in Levitin (2015, The Guardian). Poldrack et al. (2006, Neuron, 49(1), 63–74).

Low dopamine activity states alter the subjective experience of time. Five minutes of effortful, ungratifying work can feel like an hour. Two hours of stimulating, variable-reward activity — browsing, checking, processing — can feel like minutes.

This distortion makes sustained deep work experientially punishing, independent of actual difficulty. The effort is not objectively harder. It simply generates less dopaminergic activity, which the brain interprets as slower, more costly time.

Gloria Mark's field research at UC Irvine (CHI 2005, CHI 2008 — peer-reviewed) found that when workers were interrupted during tasks, it took an average of 23 minutes and 15 seconds to return to the original task, with approximately two intervening tasks occurring before resumption.

Interrupted workers reported significantly higher stress, frustration, and mental workload on NASA's Task Load Index — even when there was no significant difference in error rates. The cost is not primarily errors. It is attentional depletion and affective load.

Note: The widely-cited "10 IQ point drop from email" comes from an HP-commissioned study with 8 subjects that was not peer-reviewed. Glenn Wilson, who ran it, has called it unscientific. It has been corrected here.

Reflection Prompt

Notice what happens neurologically when you open your email or a messaging app. There is a moment of anticipation before you see what is there. That anticipation — not the content, not the outcome — is the dopamine event. The actual emails are largely irrelevant to why you keep checking.

How many times do you check a channel before you have consciously decided to? When did that start happening?

CHAPTER IV

At Scale:
Organisation, Technology & AI

The two biological mechanisms operate at the individual level. But they do not stay there. They scale into organisations through three compounding forces — and they are now being structurally accelerated by AI adoption in a way that is almost entirely unremarked upon.

1. Environmental Co-Evolution

David Kirsh's work on distributed cognition established a principle that management literature has largely ignored: when people adapt to challenging environments, they also reorganise the environment itself. They restructure workflows, tool configurations, communication norms, and physical spaces around the demands they face — embedding those demands structurally.

Applied to organisational overload: the overload does not just exist as a condition people are in. It becomes the architecture of how work is organised. Meeting cadences, reporting structures, escalation processes, and decision-rights all get configured around the assumption of overload as a baseline state. Removing overload would require structural redesign — it cannot be solved by individual habit change.

Kirsh, D. (1995). The intelligent use of space. Artificial Intelligence, 73(1-2), 31–68. · Kirsh, D. & Maglio, P. (1994). On distinguishing epistemic from pragmatic action. Cognitive Science, 18(4), 513–549.

2. The Expectation Ratchet

Each technology adoption that enables higher throughput raises the baseline expectation of throughput across the organisation. Email enabled faster communication — and created the expectation of faster responses. Slack compressed response-time expectations further. Each step raises the bar; the bar does not come back down.

Sherry Turkle observed a deeper consequence: as people are overwhelmed by information load, they come to expect more from technology and less from each other — driving further displacement of the relational and deliberative work that technology cannot substitute. The social fabric of sense-making degrades alongside the cognitive one.

3. AI Adoption — The Loop Accelerated

This is where the current moment becomes acute. The pattern of technology adoption described above is now repeating with AI — and at a speed and cognitive depth that makes the consequences more significant than previous cycles.

Gerlich (2025) conducted a mixed-method study with 666 participants across diverse age and education groups in the United Kingdom. Using the Halpern Critical Thinking Assessment with correlation and mediation analysis, the study found:

— A significant negative correlation between frequent AI usage and critical thinking ability (r = −0.75 with cognitive offloading as mediator)
— Cognitive offloading was strongly correlated with AI usage (r = +0.72)
— Younger participants (17–25) showed higher AI dependence and lower critical thinking scores
— Educational attainment partially buffered the effect

The theoretical mechanism: Cognitive load theory distinguishes between extraneous load (unnecessary effort from poor task design — reducing it is good) and germane load (the effortful engagement that produces learning, schema formation, and capability). AI tools, when used to bypass rather than scaffold effort, reduce germane load — the very load that builds capability over time.

Source: Gerlich, M. (2025). AI tools in society: Impacts on cognitive offloading and the future of critical thinking. Societies, 15(1), 6. DOI: 10.3390/soc15010006. Peer-reviewed. A correction was published September 10, 2025.

Kosmyna et al. (2025) at MIT Media Lab tracked 54 participants over four sessions using EEG brain activity measurement alongside NLP essay quality analysis. Three conditions: LLM-assisted, search-assisted, or brain-only.

Key findings:
— Brain connectivity scaled down systematically with tool support level
— LLM users showed up to 55% reduced neural connectivity vs brain-only group
— 83% of LLM users could not accurately quote their own just-written essays
— When groups were switched, LLM-to-brain users showed continued under-engagement in session 4

⚠ CRITICAL CAVEATS:

This was a preprint — not peer-reviewed at publication. Only 54 participants total; only 18 completed the 4th session. Task was narrow (SAT-style essay writing) and cannot be directly generalised. Critics note higher neural activity in brain-only group may partly reflect different motor/visual activities, not purely cognitive engagement. Treat as preliminary evidence consistent with the theoretical framework — not as settled conclusion.

Source: Kosmyna et al. (2025). Your brain on ChatGPT. arXiv:2506.08872. MIT Media Lab. Critique: Marrone & Kovanovic (2025, The Conversation).

Goldratt's Four Questions Applied

Eliyahu Goldratt proposed that technology adoption almost always fails to deliver its promised benefits because organisations change the technology while keeping the old rules that were built around the limitation the technology was supposed to remove. The four questions:

Q1 — What is the power of the technology?
AI can process, draft, summarise, and generate at a speed and volume no human can match. It removes the friction of execution for many categories of cognitive task.
Q2 — What limitation does it diminish?
The mismatch between the volume of information, decisions, and tasks in modern work and the hard ceiling of human cognitive bandwidth and time.
Q3 — What rules were built around that limitation?
That attention is finite. That fewer concurrent tasks produces better outcomes. That depth of engagement matters more than throughput. That some demands should simply not be met.
Q4 — What rules should we use now? (What is actually happening)
The rules are not changing. AI is being used to process more volume at lower engagement per item. The limitation has not been removed — it has been obscured at a cost that accumulates in cognitive capability over time.

The dopamine-AI feedback loop completes the picture. Every prompt is an uncertainty event. Every AI response is a partially unpredictable reward. The rapid, variable outputs of AI tools map closely onto the variable-ratio reinforcement schedule — the same schedule underlying slot machines and social media feeds — that generates the most sustained motivational drive.

The subjective experience of using AI to process high volumes of demands is genuinely neurologically rewarding — not because meaningful work is being accomplished, but because the prediction-error system is being continuously activated at a high frequency. The sensation of processing, navigating, and making progress is reinforced. The capacity for slower, more effortful thinking that produces genuine insight is quietly displaced.

Leadership Reflection

When your team adopts AI tools, what does success look like to you? Is it measured by output volume, response speed, or throughput? Or is it measured by the quality of judgment, the depth of thinking, and the clarity of strategic decisions?

If your metrics reward the former, your incentive structure is actively accelerating the loop described in this document — regardless of what you say about "quality" in meetings.

CHAPTER V

The Closed Loop:
Why It Keeps Repeating

The pattern repeats not because of weak management, poor culture, or individual failure of discipline. It repeats because the two biological systems at the core of it have no natural self-correcting mechanism — and every organisational and technological response to date has operated inside the loop rather than outside it.

Why Individual Willpower Cannot Break This

The loop has no natural internal correcting force. Neither of the two biological mechanisms have a satiation point in this environment. Logarithmic adaptation has no alarm that fires when you have adapted too far. The prediction-error system has no ceiling on seeking — only on consuming. And the metacognitive capacity that would detect the problem and generate the will to act has been consumed by the problem itself.

Individual willpower — the standard prescribed solution — attempts to operate inside the loop. It is itself subject to the dopamine prediction-error dynamics that make sustained effortful behaviour without immediate reward neurologically costly. And it operates inside an environment that has been structurally reorganised around the overload, as Kirsh's work describes.

The only forces that interrupt the loop are structural: changes that reduce the volume of demands at source; that protect cognitive slack as a non-negotiable design condition of how work is organised; and that redefine performance as fewer things completed with full attention rather than more things processed at lower engagement.

The Leadership and Organisational Implication

Goldratt's formulation holds exactly here. The limitation is human cognitive bandwidth. The rules built around that limitation — that attention is finite, that depth matters more than throughput, that work in progress has a cost, that some demands should not be met at all — are the rules most aggressively being discarded at the precise moment they are most important to preserve.

The question for any leader or team designing how work is done is not "how do we help people handle more?" It is: "what do we need to handle less of, so that the handling of what remains is done with full cognitive engagement?"

Adopting AI tools without answering that question first does not solve the overload. It obscures it, accelerates it, and erodes the human capacity that would eventually be needed to detect and correct it.

The technology is not the problem. Every tool discussed in this document — from email to AI — has genuine capability. The problem is the refusal to change the rules built around the limitations those tools are meant to address.

A tool that helps a person process 3× the volume of demands does not solve the overload problem if the environment responds by delivering 4× the volume of demands. It deepens it — invisibly, in the logarithmic zone, below the threshold of detection, rewarded neurologically at every step of the descent.

Seeing the loop is the prerequisite for breaking it. Everything else is navigation within it.
